Bhimrao Ramji Ambedkar

1891 - 1956

Father of Constitution

Dr. B. R. Ambedkar was popularly known as Babasaheb Ambedkar and everyone knows that he was one of the architects of the Indian Constitution. He was a very well-known political leader, eminent jurist, Buddhist activist, philosopher, anthropologist, historian, orator, writer, economist, scholar and editor, too. Dr. Ambedkar fought to eradicate the social evils like untouchability and for the rights of the dalits and other socially backward classes throughout his life. Dr. Ambedkar was appointed as India's first Law Minister in the Cabinet of Jawaharlal Nehru. He was posthumously awarded the Bharat Ratna, India's highest civilian honour in 1990.

Life Journey

  • Bhimrao Ramji Ambedkar, born on April 14, 1891, in Mhow, India, had a challenging early life marked by social discrimination. Belonging to the Mahar caste, considered untouchable in the rigid caste system, young Ambedkar faced severe prejudices. Despite the prevailing social barriers, he exhibited an unwavering determination to pursue education. Ambedkar's intellectual prowess shone through as he earned degrees in economics and law from universities in the United States and the United Kingdom. This academic journey laid the foundation for his lifelong commitment to fighting against social injustice and advocating for the rights of the marginalized communities. Ambedkar's early experiences with discrimination profoundly shaped his perspective and fueled his later endeavors to dismantle the oppressive caste system in India.

  • Bhimrao Ramji Ambedkar's educational journey was marked by resilience and determination. Despite facing discrimination as a member of the Mahar caste, considered untouchable in the Indian social hierarchy, Ambedkar displayed an early passion for learning. He obtained a scholarship to pursue higher studies at the Columbia University in the United States, where he earned a degree in economics. Furthering his academic pursuits, Ambedkar went on to attain a law degree and a doctorate from the London School of Economics. His doctoral thesis, titled "The Problem of the Rupee: Its Origin and Its Solution," showcased his intellectual prowess. Ambedkar's educational achievements not only empowered him personally but also laid the groundwork for his future role as a key architect of the Indian Constitution and a tireless advocate for social justice and equal rights.

  • B.R. Ambedkar emerged as a formidable opponent of untouchability, dedicating his life to the relentless pursuit of social justice. Having experienced firsthand the discrimination faced by the untouchables, he became a passionate advocate for their rights. Ambedkar vehemently opposed the entrenched caste system in India, arguing that untouchability was a social evil that perpetuated inequality and denied basic human dignity. His efforts to eradicate untouchability took various forms, including his participation in social and political movements, his advocacy for legal reforms, and his influential writings. Ambedkar's commitment to the cause culminated in his pivotal role in drafting the Indian Constitution, where he ensured the inclusion of provisions aimed at abolishing untouchability and promoting equality. His tireless efforts laid the foundation for the social reforms that aimed at dismantling the discriminatory practices prevalent in Indian society.

  • The Poona Pact, signed on September 24, 1932, was a significant agreement between B.R. Ambedkar, representing the untouchables, and M.K. Gandhi, representing the broader Indian society, particularly the caste Hindus. The backdrop to the pact was the Communal Award announced by the British, which allocated separate electorates for the Dalits. Fearing division along communal lines, Gandhi went on a fast to protest the award. In response, Ambedkar and Gandhi engaged in negotiations that led to the Poona Pact. The agreement abandoned separate electorates for the Dalits but reserved seats for them in the provincial legislatures, ensuring political representation without further deepening communal divisions. The Poona Pact was a crucial compromise that aimed to address the concerns of both communities and foster a more inclusive and unified approach towards social and political reforms in India.

  • B.R. Ambedkar's political career was characterized by a steadfast commitment to social justice and the empowerment of marginalized communities. He actively engaged in politics to address the deeply entrenched issues of caste discrimination in India. Ambedkar played a pivotal role in the framing of the Indian Constitution, serving as the chairman of the Drafting Committee. As India's first Law Minister, he initiated crucial legal reforms, including the Hindu Code Bill, which aimed at securing equal rights for women and lower castes. Ambedkar also founded the Scheduled Castes Federation to advance the political interests of the Dalits. Despite facing challenges and resistance, he continued to advocate for the rights of the oppressed, emphasizing the importance of education and political representation. Ambedkar's contributions to the political landscape of India were instrumental in shaping the country's commitment to equality and social justice.

  • B.R. Ambedkar's role in the drafting of the Indian Constitution is indelible and monumental. As the chairman of the Drafting Committee, he led the framing of the Constitution, ensuring it reflected the principles of justice, equality, and liberty. Ambedkar's meticulous and visionary approach was evident in his contributions to the Constitution, where he emphasized the eradication of social discrimination and the establishment of a democratic and inclusive society. His deep understanding of legal and constitutional matters, coupled with his commitment to social justice, played a crucial role in shaping the foundational document of independent India. Ambedkar's contributions included provisions for fundamental rights, safeguards for minorities, and affirmative action for the socially and economically disadvantaged sections of society. The Indian Constitution stands as a testament to Ambedkar's enduring legacy and his tireless efforts to create a democratic framework that would uphold the dignity and rights of every citizen.

  • B.R. Ambedkar's contributions to economics were marked by a keen intellect and a commitment to addressing socio-economic inequalities. He earned a doctorate in economics from the London School of Economics and wrote his thesis on the subject of the Indian currency. Ambedkar's economic ideas were influenced by his concern for the plight of the marginalized and the need for social justice. He emphasized the importance of economic and educational upliftment for the oppressed classes, advocating for land reforms, access to education, and reservations to address historical disadvantages. Ambedkar's economic philosophy was closely intertwined with his broader vision of creating a more equitable society, reflecting his belief that economic reforms were integral to achieving social and political empowerment for all sections of the population.

  • B.R. Ambedkar's marriage played a significant role in his personal life, and his approach towards matrimony was unconventional for his time. In 1906, he married Ramabai, a Chitpavan Brahmin, in what was an inter-caste marriage, defying the prevailing social norms. This decision was groundbreaking and underscored Ambedkar's commitment to challenging the caste-based discrimination deeply embedded in Indian society. Unfortunately, Ramabai faced opposition and discrimination, leading to significant hardships. Later, after her death in 1935, Ambedkar married Dr. Savita Ambedkar in 1948. Both marriages were marked by Ambedkar's forward-thinking views on social issues, especially regarding caste, and his belief in the transformative power of personal choices to challenge ingrained prejudices.

  • B.R. Ambedkar's conversion to Buddhism was a profound and symbolic step, reflecting his quest for social justice and rejection of the caste system. On October 14, 1956, Ambedkar, along with thousands of his followers, embraced Buddhism in a mass conversion ceremony in Nagpur, India. This momentous event was a deliberate departure from Hinduism, which Ambedkar viewed as perpetuating social inequalities. By embracing Buddhism, he sought a path that aligned with his vision of equality, liberty, and fraternity. Ambedkar's conversion to Buddhism was not merely a personal spiritual choice; it was a public declaration against the social injustices inherent in the caste system and an affirmation of his commitment to building a more egalitarian society based on Buddhist principles. This conversion had a lasting impact, inspiring many followers to adopt Buddhism as a way of rejecting the discriminatory practices associated with their former caste status.

  • B.R. Ambedkar's death on December 6, 1956, marked the end of a remarkable life dedicated to the pursuit of social justice and equality. Despite facing numerous challenges throughout his life, Ambedkar left an enduring legacy as a key architect of the Indian Constitution and a champion for the rights of the marginalized. His contributions to law, politics, and social reform continue to shape modern India. Ambedkar's passing was a significant loss, but his ideas and principles have continued to influence generations, inspiring movements for equality and justice. His commitment to eradicating caste-based discrimination and ensuring the rights and dignity of all citizens remains a guiding force in India's ongoing journey toward a more inclusive and egalitarian society.
